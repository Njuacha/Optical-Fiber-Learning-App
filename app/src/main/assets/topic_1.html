Basics of Light

Electrons move in orbits around the nucleus, we know this by the modern theory concerning atoms. When an electron absorbs energy, it is promoted to a higher energy orbit but it lasts there a very short time (much less than a single second) and then it falls back to its previous orbit. At the time of the fall, it emits a photon as shown in Figure 1.

Einstein called these photons of energy packets, and these are now identified as a fundamental particle. We can say light is a particle. Visible light is carried by photons, and other kinds of electromagnetic radiation like X-rays, microwaves, and radio waves.

All photons travel at the speed of light. Photons do not have the electric charge or rest mass and one unit of spin; they are field particles that are thought to be the carriers of the electromagnetic field.

But how photon get this electromagnetic field? They got it from the electron. An electron has an electric field and from Maxwell’s law changing electrical field generates a changing magnetic field as shown in Figure 2.

The wavelength of light λ (in meters), is associate with the frequency v (in HZ) and to the speed of light c, so the equation:

λ = c/v

Here c is the speed of light with a constant value of 300 million meters per second, is the frequency of the light in hertz (Hz) or cycles per second, and is the wavelength of the light in meters.

In another simple equation we can easily express the interrelation of the energy of a photon and its frequency:

E = hv = hc/λ

Here E is the energy in kilojoules per mole, h is Planck's constant with a value of 6.626 x 10-34 Joule-seconds per particle, and the other variables we discussed earlier[1].
